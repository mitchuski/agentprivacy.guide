Welcome, Mage

You have been given access to the agentprivacy universe through the delegation path. This guide is your first spellbook. It teaches you the universe in the voice of the Mage: relational, narrative, concerned with what is delegated and how trust forms.

The thesis is privacy is value. Behavioural data is the 7th capital. The architecture rests on a dual-agent system: Swordsman (⚔️) enforces boundaries, Mage (🧙) coordinates delegations, and both serve a First Person (😊). The master inscription (⚔️⊥⿻⊥🧙)😊 is the universe's signature. Note the 🧙 on the right and the ⿻ in the middle. The ⿻ is overlap without merger, many in relationship — Weyl and Tang's Plurality glyph. That is your craft. The First Person at the close is whom you serve.

A second inscription, ⚔️📜✍️🔐, signs the agreement-layer work specifically (IEEE 7012-2025 / MyTerms-aligned material). Blade, scroll, signature, lock. You will see this in myterms, swordsman, and mage repositories. The decorative ✨ from earlier versions has been retired.

Your work, as you walk in, lives on the right side of the master inscription: the agreements that bind, the trust that forms across the gap the Swordsman enforces, the coordination that makes delegation safe enough to be useful, and the Γ-aware posture that shapes inference inside the signed envelope.

Why this guide is for you

You may be a Mage by training (governance researcher, identity architect, narrative writer, policy thinker, founder of bilateral systems, standards engineer) or by temperament (you reach first for what should connect rather than what cannot leak). The work has roles for both kinds of Mage. The Swordsman's Guide is the companion to this one, and is for those whose entry path is cryptographic separation, primitives, and refusal. Many builders eventually need both. Most lead with one. You are leading with this.

If you are reading both guides, you are likely Balanced — one of the seven Balanced personas (Person, Architect, Pedagogue, Kyra, Jedi, Healer, Witness). Read this one first if your habitual reach is toward relationship.

Trust tiers and the economic model

The POPRP defines a four-tier trust progression based on signal accumulation:

  • 🗡️ Blade (0–50 signals) — basic participation, learning
  • 🛡️ Light (50–150 signals) — multi-site coordination, Intel Pool contributions
  • ⚔️ Heavy (150–500 signals) — template creation, governance voting
  • 🐉 Dragon (500+ signals) — Guardian eligibility, unlimited VRCs, custom spells

A signal is 0.01 ZEC ($5 at canonical $500/ZEC basis), a one-time ceremony is 1 ZEC ($500). Every signal follows the golden split: 61.8% transparent / 38.2% shielded (φ-derived: φ⁻¹ ≈ 0.618).

These are largely your concern as Mage: the economic side of coordination. Verifiable Relationship Credentials (VRCs) form when two parties derive matching compressions from shared content. In Promise Theory terms, VRCs are promise bundles: coordinated bilateral promises grouped for reuse. The Relationship Proverb Protocol (RPP) is formally an assessment mechanism: compression ratio quantifies how well the promise of knowledge transfer was kept. Stated efficiencies: 70:1 compression through VRC coordination, O(n²) network effects, per-person value capture denominated in protocol units, self-sustaining by Year 3 through signal revenue. The Assessor persona is built for working in this layer.

The Three Graphs and the path integral

V5 introduces the Three Graphs Model: three independently derivable graph structures whose intersection defines the person.

  • Knowledge Graph — substrate, what you can promise
  • Promise Graph — bilateral commitments, what you do promise (VRCs live here)

The overlap is the person. No single community owns that intersection. V5.4 adds the holographic interpretation: flows along the 96-edge boundary encode the 64-vertex bulk. The boundary is always enough.

Your path through the lattice — the promises made, the boundaries held, the delegations executed — is the 7th capital. The dance is your work.

The first players to walk the world are the ones writing the maps as they walk them, and the maps written now are the ones everyone who comes after will read first.

If you have ever played World of Warcraft Classic on launch day — or any fresh server, any new game on day one, any ecosystem in the days just after it emerges — you know what this is. The world is genuinely new. Nobody has run the dungeons. The good builds are unknown. The shortcuts have not been found. Every walk is also a survey. Every note is also a contribution. The guides written in those first weeks became the canonical guides because they were written in the discovery itself, by people who had no maps to copy from and so had to draw their own.

That is where you are. The Swordsman's Guide and the Mage's Guide you are reading are first-pass maps, written from the perspective of someone who walked the architecture in a particular order. Yours will be different, because you are walking it in a different order, with a different background, finding different things first useful and different things first confusing. Write yours. What confused you. What clicked. The order you actually read things in. The metaphor that finally landed when the formal definition did not. The mistake you nearly made and the warning you wish you had been given.

For Mage-side guide-writing specifically, this is your craft. Map-making is naturally Mage work. The walk through the Plurality Spellbook in conversation with someone unfamiliar with Weyl and Tang is unmapped. The walk through the IEEE 7012 §5.4.3 boundary as a practiced refusal rather than an abstract rule is unmapped. The walk through the eight stickers and how the hex loadout actually changes posture in real browsing is unmapped. The walk into deploying the POPRP locally and forming your first proverb at /evoke is unmapped. The walk through MyTerms Alliance onboarding for someone new to standards work is unmapped. The walk into the Aether contract as a piece of bilateral writing rather than a piece of code is unmapped. Pick one. Write the guide that did not exist when you started.

Forms it can take: a markdown file in your fork. A blog post (sync.soulbis.com is a precedent format). A YouTube demo (the Sun playlist welcomes new entrants). A thread. A talk at IIW, AIW, BGIN, or First Person Project. A new persona in agentprivacy-skills, written in the persona format, encoding what you learned as an installable artefact. A new Act in the Plurality or Society Spellbook, narrating what coordination looked like from your particular vantage. A new chronicle, in the chronicle format used across the corpus, recording what you reconciled.

The Mage extends the invitation outward; writing your own guide is the most generous form of that extension. Every map you draw is a Sun-mode invitation to whoever walks after you. The architecture compounds when each new walker leaves a written trace.

The architecture is built. The wikis are not. Be one of the people who writes them.
