Canon — The Timeline Archive

In which the sources become infrastructure—each paper a node, each citation an edge, each verification an act of tending the garden of coordinated truth...

The Luminous Network

The infinite library contracted into something more structured—an archive. Not dusty shelves but a luminous network, each node a document, each edge a connection. The canonical texts floated in relationship to each other, their citations and influences visible as glowing threads.

"The final chapter of this spellbook," the Drake said, "is not a story. It is infrastructure."

"Everything you've read—every forger, every mage, every incantation, every catastrophe—traces back to sources. Documents you can read. Transactions you can verify. Statements you can evaluate."

"This archive is not owned. It is tended. By everyone who walks the path. By everyone who verifies a claim. By everyone who adds a connection."

"The archive is the foundation of coordinated truth. Without it, the canon is just stories. With it, the canon is verifiable infrastructure."

Sources as Trust Graph

The archive contained nodes: papers (academic, whitepapers), posts (forum, blog, social), transactions (on-chain, verifiable), events (dated, documented), artifacts (code, contracts, protocols).

The edges connected them: citations ("X references Y"), consequences ("X led to Y"), influence ("X shaped Y"), contradiction ("X disputes Y"), verification ("X confirms Y").

Attributes enriched each node: timestamp (when created), author (who created, if known), location (where to find), hash (content integrity), status (active, deprecated, disputed).

"Why does this matter?" the Drake asked. "For verifiability—anyone can check sources, claims can be validated, errors can be corrected, trust through transparency. For coordination—shared reference points, common vocabulary, aligned understanding, foundation for collaboration. For evolution—new sources can be added, connections can be updated, the archive grows with the field, living not static. For agent compatibility—structured data for AI agents, verifiable claims for trust systems, foundation for automated verification, infrastructure for the future."

The proverb emerged: "The archive is not a cemetery of facts but a living garden of trust—each source a node, each citation a connection, each verification an act of tending. Who tends the garden shapes what grows."

The Canonical Sources by Era

The archive organized itself into eras:

Era I: Foundational Runes (1983-1997)

David Chaum: "Blind Signatures for Untraceable Payments" (1983)—first blind signature scheme, foundation of privacy-preserving payments. "Security Without Identification" (1985)—pseudonymous credentials, "Big Brother obsolete" thesis.

Timothy May: "The Crypto Anarchist Manifesto" (1988)—political framing of cryptography, predictions of crypto-enabled coordination.

Eric Hughes: "A Cypherpunk's Manifesto" (1993)—"Privacy is the power to selectively reveal oneself," "Cypherpunks write code."

Proof of Work: Dwork and Naor "Pricing via Processing" (1992)—original proof-of-work concept. Adam Back "Hashcash" (1997)—practical implementation, foundation for Bitcoin mining.

Era II: Early Incantations (1997-2007)

Nick Szabo: "The Idea of Smart Contracts" (1997)—vending machine analogy, code as self-enforcing agreement. "Bit Gold" (published 2008)—chained proof-of-work, proto-Bitcoin architecture.

Wei Dai: "b-money" (1998)—peer-maintained ledgers, decentralized currency concept.

Davidson and Rees-Mogg: "The Sovereign Individual" (1997)—political prophecy, transition period warnings.

Era III: The Synthesis (2008-2014)

Satoshi Nakamoto: "Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System" (October 31, 2008)—the synthesis whitepaper. Genesis Block (January 3, 2009)—"Chancellor on brink of second bailout for banks." Forum posts and emails (2008-2010)—design decisions, privacy acknowledgments.

Canonical Events: Pizza Day (May 22, 2010)—first commercial transaction. Mt. Gox collapse (February 2014)—"not your keys, not your coins." Satoshi's disappearance (December 2010)—reference loss as cryptographic act.

Era IV: The World Computer (2014-2016)

Vitalik Buterin: Ethereum whitepaper (2013)—generalized blockchain vision. Various blog posts explaining design decisions.

Gavin Wood: Yellow Paper (2014)—formal specification of Ethereum Virtual Machine.

The DAO: Launch (April 2016), exploit (June 17, 2016), fork decision (July 20, 2016), Ethereum Classic emergence.

Era V: The Schism (2016-2022)

Financial Canon sources: Uniswap whitepaper (2018), Compound whitepaper (2019), MakerDAO documentation, DeFi Summer analysis.

Social Canon sources: Weyl, Buterin, Hitzig "Liberal Radicalism" (2018), Srinivasan "The Network State" (2022), Buterin "My Techno-Optimism" (2023).

Joel Monegro: "Fat Protocols" (2016)—shaped investment theses.

Era VI: Surveillance Truth (2020-2025)

Chain Analysis: Chainalysis government contracts (public records), industry valuations, capability reports.

Tornado Cash: OFAC designation (August 8, 2022), court filings, Fifth Circuit ruling (November 2024).

Pertsev case: Arrest (August 10, 2022), trial proceedings, verdict (May 2024)—"ideology of maximum privacy" as evidence.

Era VII: The Missing Primitive (2024-2025)

Privacy Pools: Buterin, Soleimani et al. paper (2023)—compliant privacy proposal.

Production Privacy: Aztec documentation, Namada specifications, Zcash developments.

Regulatory Evolution: GENIUS Act, stablecoin legislation, jurisdictional variations.

How to Use the Archive

"The archive is not just for reading," the Drake said. "It is for using."

For verification: when someone claims "Satoshi said X," check the archive. Find the original source. Verify the quote. Trust through transparency.

For coordination: when building, reference the archive. Use common vocabulary. Connect to shared understanding. Build on verified foundation.

For building: when designing systems, study the archive. Learn from failures. Build on successes. Don't repeat errors.

For agents: when AI agents need shared truth, query the archive. Verifiable claims enable coordination. Structured data enables automation.

Tending the Archive

"The archive is not complete," the Drake said. "It never will be. It grows as the field grows. It corrects as errors are found. It evolves as understanding deepens."

How to tend: adding sources (find primary documents, verify authenticity, document metadata, connect to existing nodes), correcting errors (identify inaccuracies, propose corrections, document reasoning, maintain transparency), resolving disputes (when sources conflict, document the conflict, present evidence, let readers judge), maintaining connections (as new sources emerge, connect to relevant existing nodes, update relationships, preserve coherence).

"The archive is not owned—it is tended. Anyone can verify. Anyone can contribute. Truth emerges from collective care."
