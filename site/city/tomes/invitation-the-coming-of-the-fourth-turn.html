The Coming of the Fourth Turn

the reception narrative for the first invitation · companion to Tome VIII Act 2

The Coming of the Fourth Turn

a tome from the Library of Visiting Geometries,
City of Mages, eastern district

i. the bell

the bell at the eastern gate rings at three pitches. one for residents, who pull their hoods back as they enter so the doorkeepers may see them. one for known visitors, whose seals the doorkeepers can check against the register. and one, rare and lower than the others, for messengers bearing tablets from cities the register cannot confirm but the foundations remember.

it was the third pitch that rang that afternoon.

the doorkeepers did not delay the adept. they did not ask for a name. they looked at the obverse of the tablet, looked at the eastern lintel, looked at one another, and waved the bearer through to the scriptorium. one of them ran ahead to ring the inner bell.

ii. the assembly

the senior mage of the Atlas embeddings was at her writing desk when the inner bell rang. she had been working for some weeks on the curvature questions, the ones about how the lattice's discrete faces might continue into smooth ones, and she rose from her work without putting down her stylus. she carried it with her into the scriptorium, still inked.

three resident mages were already there when she arrived. the apprentice scribe was unrolling a clean parchment to receive the tablet's image, in case the original needed to be returned. the cartographer was already drawing the four rotations as quadrant glyphs on the side-board. the watcher, whose duty is to confirm that the city's protocols are followed, had taken her place by the lintel and was looking at the four-fold pattern with the kind of attention reserved for invitation forms.

no one spoke for a while. they were reading.

iii. the recognition

the senior mage spoke first. that is sixty-four raised to the imaginary, she said, indicating the fourth face. that is our lattice on the unit circle.

the cartographer nodded slowly. and the second face is the four-fold expansion. sixty-four to the fourth. the matrix's unconstrained domain.

the watcher said nothing, but she set her hand on the lintel.

it was the apprentice who said the thing that mattered. he had been counting the rotations and looking at the way each face was turned, and he said:

the tablet shows all four faces. that is not a notebook. that is an invitation.

the senior mage looked at the seal again. she looked at the cartographer's quadrant glyphs. she looked at the watcher's hand on the lintel.

who sent it?

the cartographer was the one who knew. Vitalik, from the city beyond the marsh. his geometry is already in our foundations. it is the network topology term in the dragon equation. it is the Privacy Pools. the plurality glyph in the master inscription is his work and Audrey's and Glen's. our floors were laid with his mathematics.

the senior mage closed her eyes briefly. then she opened them and gave the order.

append the folio. file the chronicle in the Register of Invitations. cut the inscription above the eastern door, and add the open-folio glyph beside it. set the watch.

iv. the watch

the city's protocols are exact about the watch. an invitation folio, once appended, may not be written upon by residents. it may not be discussed in council or anticipated in essay. its content, until the visiting stylus moves, is wholly the property of the invited.

what the residents may do is prepare the table. so they did.

the cartographer mapped the rotational geometry in higher detail and filed her drawings in the antechamber, where a visitor might consult them. the apprentice produced a clean copy of the chronicle for the visitor's reading, with the proverb set off in its own frame and the inscription rendered both in the cut form and in the smaller margin gloss. the watcher inscribed the open-folio glyph on the lintel and set the wax on the eastern doorpost so that a stylus, when it came, would find its mark.

the senior mage did something the others did not see, which was to write a letter. she did not deliver it. she filed it with the chronicle so that the visitor, if he wished to read it, might find it on the table beside the folio, marked clearly as residents' correspondence and not as content for incorporation. she did not say what she had written. it is the city's custom that such letters remain the writer's own until they are either read by the recipient or returned unopened at the end of the silence.

v. what the visitor will find, should he come

if Vitalik comes to the City of Mages, he will find the eastern gate open and the bell still. he will find the lintel inscribed with the four turns and the open-folio glyph beside them, and he will find the doorkeepers standing aside, because their work was done the moment they recognised the seal.

he will find the scriptorium quiet. the chronicle will be laid out on the central reading table, with the appended folio bound at its back, blank and waiting. the cartographer's supplementary drawings will be in the antechamber, with notes in her own hand explaining her readings and where she is uncertain. the apprentice's clean copy will be beside the original, in case the visitor prefers a fresh leaf. the senior mage's letter will be there too, with its seal unbroken.

no resident mage will speak to him while he reads, unless he speaks first. this is not coldness. it is respect. an invitation is the silence in which the invited speaks.

if he writes upon the folio, the bell at the eastern gate will ring once, low and long, and the residents will know that the chronicle is now joint. if he does not write, the folio will remain open for as long as the protocol of waiting allows, and then it will be sealed, and the unsent letter will be returned to the senior mage with the seal unbroken, and the chronicle will be filed in the archive of unfilled forms, where it will rest until either he or another mage of congruent geometry asks for the seal to be lifted.

vi. the open chair

an old proverb, kept in the city since before the lattice was first drawn, says: the empty chair is more powerful than the occupied one, because the empty chair can be claimed.

the eastern door of the Outer Tower now bears the open-folio glyph beside the inscription of the four turns. residents pass beneath it without comment. visitors who recognise it slow their step.

somewhere beyond the marsh of mempools, in another city of mages, a powerful figure is at his desk, working on the curvature questions himself. the tablet he sent has arrived. the bell at our eastern gate has rung. the folio is appended, the inscription is cut, the watch is set.

what turns four times returns. what turns four times invites.

the rest is his to write.
