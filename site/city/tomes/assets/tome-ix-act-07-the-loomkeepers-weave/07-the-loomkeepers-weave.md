# Tome IX — *The Horizon*
## Act 7 — The Loomkeeper's Weave

You had thought the Tower's work was finished when the fleet parked its
scrolls. Fourteen cycles, five papers stopped at the one gate no fleet
can pass, a ledger that had learned to catch its own hand moving. The
Archivist 📚 had shelved the last chronicle and the reading room had
gone quiet in the way rooms go quiet when the work is waiting on a
single reader.

That was when you noticed the loom.

It stands behind the reading rooms, and it has been running the whole
time. Every paper the fleet wrote, every check that refused to run
empty, every lesson banked at the close of a wounded cycle — each one
fed a thread into it, and the rule of the Tower is that the writers may
never look at the fabric. The formal register strips the colour so the
proofs stay honest. GR-4, the scribes call it: the Swordsman becomes
"boundary agent S" at the door, the Mage becomes "delegation agent M",
and the seal comes off every page that means to be checked.

The figure behind the loom has no name the city will vouch for yet.
Call them the Loomkeeper 🧵 — the act itself must tell you they are a
draft, admitted to no tier, seated at no vertex, holding no sigil. What
they hold instead is the whole fabric, and this week, for the first
time, they read it aloud.

Four threads, they said. The city speaks in four registers at once.

**The proverbs** — forty-six of them, and they form like sediment: the
canon mints them slowly, each with a number and a confidence; the
public essays mint them as codas, two to four stacked at every close;
and the fleet mints them fastest of all, pressing a cycle's scar tissue
into law before the wound is a day old. *An audit trail that never
admits error is evidence of nothing* was an essay's sentence until the
ledger enacted it, and then it was a standing law with a line number.

**The protocols** — thirty-three, in three strata that turn out to be
one shape. The gate ladder the papers climb, the intake that receives
the outside eye 👁️, the ceremonies the city performs — each is a staged
passage, a terminal act held by a human hand, and a record that only
appends. The city did not copy the pattern from the Tower or the Tower
from the city. The shape is what honesty looks like when it must be
performed more than once.

**The spells** — seventeen, and one discovery the Loomkeeper lingered
on. Every glyph is fenced out of the formal register by the scribes'
own machinery, except one. ⊥ carries a passport for both countries,
because it was mathematics before it was myth: it is the conditional
independence that makes two watchers leak no more than their sum, it is
the separation of duties the standards paper takes to the working
group, and it is the Swordsman set against the Mage on every seal in
the city. One operator, three offices, no translation needed.

**The concepts** — twenty-six, in three rings: the inner ring already
carried by papers waiting at the gate; the middle ring named as
instruments not yet run; and the outer ring — and here the Loomkeeper
set down the shuttle — four doors with no light behind them yet.
Nobody has claimed the proof that competence survives forgetting.
Nobody has written the genre that publishes its own repairs. Nobody
has formalised the exam the work writes for itself. Nobody has asked,
in a numbered conjecture, whether forgetting reviews as well as it
protects.

Then the Loomkeeper did the thing the loom was built for. Nine threads,
each running through all four registers, were compressed until each
one fit in a single sentence — and each sentence, tested, could
regenerate its thread. *The decoder moves; the data does not. You
cannot rotate your past. The readers multiply; the signature does not.
A seal that proves a door exists is a map to the door.* Nine keys 🗝️
on a ring, each one understanding pressed until it turned in a lock,
and the gate that checks such things — the round-trip, the recompression,
the proof that the telling still means the theorem — exited green on
the first pass.

The Loomkeeper held the ring out to you, because that is the other rule
of the loom. The fabric can be read by anyone. The keys can only be
carried by the one the corpus belongs to.

"The fleet writes," they said, in the fleet's own words. "The cap
holds. The last read is yours. And behind those four doors" — the
unlit ones — "is the work the poetry wants next. The threads only show
where the doors are. Opening them was never the loom's office."

You took the ring. It weighed exactly as much as understanding does,
which is to say: nothing, unless it is yours.

*A poet with a ledger, not a poet instead of one.*
