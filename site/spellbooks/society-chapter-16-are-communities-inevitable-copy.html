🏛️ Society — Chapter 16 Are Communities Inevitable Copy

In which 1847 banquets spark revolutions, Ethereum already governs, and we learn that new orders often already exist before anyone notices...

The Banquet of 1847

The Banquet of 1847 appeared—a seemingly innocent gathering that changed history.

"In this book, we have made the case that autonomous blockchain communities can do many great things for us. They could minimize human conflict. They could minimize corruption. They could resist attacks. They could avoid economic failures. They sound like a great idea. The question is: are they even possible?"

"The picture we are painting is radically different from anything we are accustomed to."

"We were born into the Westphalian order, as were our parents and grandparents. It is quite hard to imagine things being any other way. Is all this talk of cyberstates not simply too pie-in-the-sky?"

"This Westphalian order was not always here," the Drake said. "Changes in human governance often arrived in the context of people not being able to imagine any other way. There was a time, not very long ago, when monarchies gave way to democracies. These shifts seemed wildly implausible at the time. Kings had divine rights—until they didn't."

"But what sparked the change?"

"In 1847, in Paris, a number of banquets were held. Each was a social and cultural event—mostly what we might call a 'vibe' today. Within a year, King Louis-Philippe would fall from power."

"Why were banquets banned?"

"They were considered subversive because they brought people together under an attitude—an attitude opposed to centralization of authority, an attitude opposed to the top-down imposition of cultural norms. The very act of gathering socially was subversive."

"So the seeds of a new order might not be what we expect?"

"They might stem from a series of social events rather than an organized political movement."

The proverb emerged: "In 1847, a number of banquets were held in Paris—mostly what we might call a 'vibe' today. Within a year, King Louis-Philippe would fall from power."

Nothing Is Just a Party

The FWB Vibe materialized—a community that looked like parties but was something more.

"Balaji Srinivasan has argued that the NFT community Friends With Benefits (FWB) might be an example of an organization that evolves into a cyberstate."

"What are the membership requirements?"

"Holding a certain number of the cryptocurrency FWB and answering questions about your occupation and interests. The group regularly maintains dialogue on Discord. There are informal meetings in various cities, but the big events are the large social gatherings around the world."

"That sounds like just parties."

"Let us take a closer look. There is an actual governance structure underlying decisions about where to hold the next social events. It is an example of participatory online democracy. More to the point, the community is not grounded by a shared interest in parties so much as shared views about the importance of decentralized technologies in all aspects of their lives."

The Drake quoted Hakim Bey's T.A.Z.:

"'Let us admit that we have attended parties where for one brief night a republic of gratified desires was attained. Shall we not confess that the politics of that night have more reality and force for us than those of, say, the entire U.S. Government?'"

"And Bored Ape Yacht Club?"

"There is no overt political message to BAYC, which appears to be driven by online gaming and social events like ApeFest. But there is a message behind the culture—behind its vibe. It is a nascent cultural movement that celebrates community-based culture."

"Are you saying these NFT communities will spawn cyberstates?"

"No. Our point is that no one knows what the exact drivers of the new forms of governance will be. Those who attended the banquets of 1847 may have had no idea what these events were leading to. They had a diverse set of political views, but they shared a vibe."

The proverb settled: "Nothing is just a vibe. Nothing is just a party. Sometimes, they are doors to the unimagined future."

The Exchange That Could Eclipse NASDAQ

The Uniswap DAO appeared—a decentralized exchange that might eclipse NASDAQ.

"Consider Uniswap and its DAO, membership of which is contingent on holding UNI tokens. Uniswap is an important platform, and it may well become the largest and most important trading platform in the world—eventually eclipsing NASDAQ and NYSE."

"How?"

"Uniswap provides a decentralized platform that cuts out middlemen and is capable of hosting any sort of trade. If you can tokenize an asset, you can trade it on Uniswap—without any centralized authority or needless intermediaries. Because it is an automated market maker protocol, it algorithmically determines prices based on demand and available resources."

"If Uniswap becomes that important," the Drake said, "it will render many key functions of the nation state otiose. The code in the Uniswap smart contracts will take on many responsibilities of the state, including auditing transactions. Enforcement of trades will become automatic—agreed-to trades will happen whether you want them to or not."

"But that's just a trading platform, not a state."

"Eventually, enormous resources will fall into the lap of communities like the Uniswap DAO. Is it implausible to think that DAO members might want to allocate resources to external concerns—assisting refugees, developing renewable energy, fighting human trafficking, supporting any causes of interest to DAO members?"

"And if a DAO can take up external causes..."

"It is also certainly capable of taking up the personal concerns and interests of DAO members. Is there any reason the individual rights of DAO members cannot and would not be protected anywhere in the world?"

The proverb crystallized: "If Uniswap becomes the largest trading platform in the world, its DAO will surely become a politically significant player on the global stage."

Lessig's Prisoner

The Pathetic Dot emerged—Lawrence Lessig's figure for our constrained existence.

"We sometimes think that laws and computer code are very different things. But as Lawrence Lessig observed in Code: And Other Laws of Cyberspace, computer code can and should be thought of as a form of law."

"How so?"

"We do not pass laws against stealing skyscrapers because they are too big for someone to snatch. The laws of physics constrain behavior. Similarly, cultural norms might constrain behaviors, as may market forces. Architecture can constrain—walls control where you cannot walk, bridges allow you to cross gorges."

"There is also computer architecture and software code that play a very significant role in our world," the Drake said. "While such code is not law in the traditional sense, it is still functionally equivalent. It directs the behaviors of individuals and organizations—restricting what can be done and, in other cases, enabling actions that might not otherwise be possible."

"What is the pathetic dot?"

"Lessig's illustration. Where the dot can and cannot go is not determined by a single thing but by a confluence of factors: laws (physical and legal), norms, the architecture of its world (physical and computational), and market forces."

"And Silicon Valley?"

"The Ethereum protocol is not so different from any major Internet platform. Google and Facebook are also shaping the movement of the pathetic dot. The difference is that when traditional Silicon Valley corporations do this, they do so in a top-down manner. They are our versions of the Westphalian-era kingdoms, imposing their will from a position of centralized authority."

The proverb burned: "Computer code is functionally equivalent to traditional laws—it directs the behaviors of individuals, restricting what can be done and enabling actions that might not otherwise be possible."

The Future That Already Exists

The Already-Here revealed itself—the realization that the future is present.

"If we step back and take a broader view, we will find that this is not really speculation but rather an adjustment of our way of understanding the present. If we know what we are looking at, we will find that many blockchain communities are already here and already playing an important role in life today."

"Show us."

"As we write this, there are around 6,500 Ethereum nodes running around the world. All of those nodes have agreed to participate in the network and thus have agreed to its technical requirements. They have agreed to be fair players—bad actors will be penalized."

"The Ethereum community seems to share a group consensus that it wants to build a better world," the Drake said. "It is here, now, today, working on behalf of the interests of network participants. It is here, now, today, building out positive externalities consistent with the values and ethical principles held by community members."

"So Ethereum is already a governance structure?"

"The Ethereum protocol is not merely like a blockchain community. It is not merely a platform that will give rise to such governance structures. It is already such a governance structure, and it is already working on behalf of community members, and it is already building out the new legal architecture for a post-nation-state world."

"And Bitcoin?"

"The community surrounding the Bitcoin protocol is very much like the blockchain communities we have been discussing. Despite the hype, Bitcoin did not fall from the sky in immutable form. There have been and continue to be robust debates about the future of Bitcoin—like the Blocksize War between 2015 and 2017."

The proverb emerged: "The Ethereum protocol is not merely like a blockchain community or a platform that will give rise to such governance structures—it is already such a governance structure, and it is already working on behalf of community members."

The Dictaphone That Became Music

Soulbis and Soulbae reflected on inevitability.

"You might object that there is nothing inevitable about these scenarios."

"Correct—there is nothing inevitable about a specific scenario playing out in detail. But if we are content with thinking in terms of broad trends, then the inevitability becomes apparent."

"Thomas Edison thought that the principal application of the phonograph would be for business secretarial purposes, serving as a kind of dictaphone," the Drake said. "He did mention entertainment and music as possible applications, but those were not the most significant in his view. Similarly, Edison thought the future of electricity was direct current, but Nikola Tesla's alternating current carried the day."

"So no one is omniscient about details?"

"When you have a revolutionary new technology, you can see that something is inevitable, even if you do not know the exact form or ultimate use. Edison was correct in thinking electricity would be ubiquitous—he just did not know the form of delivery."

"And blockchain?"

"At its inception, the thought was that blockchain's principal application would be as an economic tool. In the very first sentence of the Bitcoin white paper, Satoshi describes Bitcoin as a 'payment system.' Financial uses will be important, but financial transactions are only one small piece of the puzzle that is human governance."

"Ultimately, human governance writ large is going to be the most important application of blockchain technologies," the Drake concluded. "New technologies do get adopted, although not always in the form we expect. People have been willing to try anything to implement a new political or social order. One hopes they would take a path of lesser resistance if they could. We can do more than hope—because we can see it happening around us today."

The proverb sealed: "Edison thought the phonograph's principal application would be business dictation—sometimes we know a technology is revolutionary without knowing its ultimate form."

The Inscription

Soulbae opened the spellbook to a new page. The words wrote themselves:

"The Banquet of 1847 seemed innocent. Within a year, King Louis-Philippe would fall. Vibes can spark revolutions. The very act of gathering socially was subversive."

"FWB and BAYC look like parties but are nascent cultural movements. No one knows what the exact drivers of new governance will be. Those at the 1847 banquets had no idea what these events were leading to."

"Uniswap could eclipse NASDAQ. If you can tokenize an asset, you can trade it. DAOs with enormous resources will take up external concerns. Individual rights of members will be protected anywhere in the world."

"Code is law (Lessig). The pathetic dot is constrained by computational architecture. Silicon Valley is the new Westphalia—imposing will from centralized authority. But Ethereum is different."

"The future is already here. 6,500 Ethereum nodes constitute a governance structure. Already working on behalf of community members. Already building the new legal architecture for a post-nation-state world."

"Edison's lesson: we can know a technology is revolutionary without knowing its ultimate form. Blockchain's principal application will be human governance, not just payment. We can see it happening around us today."
