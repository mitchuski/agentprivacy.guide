🏛️ Society — Chapter 08 The Cyberstate Question

In which John Perry Barlow declares independence, Balaji dreams of network states, and we learn that you don't need land to have power—nor recognition from dying empires to matter...

The Rancher's Declaration

Barlow's Ghost materialized—wearing cowboy boots and carrying a manifesto that had once electrified the early Internet.

"In 1996, after the United States Congress passed the Communications Decency Act, I—John Perry Barlow, Republican Wyoming cattle rancher, former lyricist for the Grateful Dead, and cofounder of the Electronic Frontier Foundation—wrote and uploaded my proclamation."

"What did it say?" Soulbae asked.

"'A Declaration of the Independence of Cyberspace.' From its opening paragraph, I did not pull punches:

'Governments of the Industrial World, you weary giants of flesh and steel, I come from Cyberspace, the new home of Mind. On behalf of the future, I ask you of the past to leave us alone. You are not welcome among us. You have no sovereignty where we gather.'"

"You told nation-states they had no claim over cyberspace."

"I doubled down:

'Governments derive their just powers from the consent of the governed. You have neither solicited nor received ours. We did not invite you. You do not know us, nor do you know our world. Cyberspace does not lie within your borders. Do not think that you can build it, as though it were a public construction project. You cannot. It is an act of nature and it grows itself through our collective actions.'"

"The declaration concluded with a pledge to create a 'civilization of the Mind' in cyberspace," the Drake said. "'We must declare our virtual selves immune to your sovereignty.' 'We will spread ourselves across the Planet so that no one can arrest our thoughts.' And the aspiration: 'May our Civilization of the Mind be more humane and fair than the world your governments have made before.'"

"What happened to that dream?" Soulbis asked.

The Island Keeper appeared—carrying essays from twenty years of reflection.

"I am from Peter Ludlow's 2001 collection, Crypto Anarchy, Cyberstates, and Pirate Utopias. It asked: What if virtual states could be formed that existed online—cyberstates? Would it be possible? Would it be a good thing?"

"And the answer?"

"Ludlow concluded such states were indeed possible and already forming. But he expressed pessimism about their ability to persist. Terrestrial governments would surely use their power to eliminate them eventually. He closed his anthology with Hakim Bey's underground manifesto, T.A.Z.: The Temporary Autonomous Zone."

"Temporary?"

"Bey thought these islands in the net, like the Caribbean pirate enclaves of the eighteenth century, would be fleeting postmodern utopias. They would form, dissolve, and then reorganize elsewhere."

"Ludlow's volume initially 'fell stillborn from the press,' as David Hume once said of his most famous publication," the Drake noted. "But more than two decades later, with the rise of social media and web3 and blockchain technologies, people have begun to revisit its central thesis. Are cyberstates a possibility after all? And is the outlook for their persistence more optimistic than it was two decades ago?"

The proverb emerged: "A cattle rancher and Grateful Dead lyricist told the governments of the world to leave cyberspace alone—and for a moment, it seemed possible."

Balaji's Network State

Balaji's Vision appeared—carrying a book that had captured Silicon Valley's imagination.

"I am Balaji Srinivasan's definition of a 'network state.' Let me give you the one-sentence version:

'A network state is a highly aligned online community with a capacity for collective action that crowdfunds territory around the world and eventually gains diplomatic recognition from pre-existing states.'"

"Three components," Soulbae noted:

  1. A highly aligned online community with capacity for collective action
  2. Crowdfunding territory around the world
  3. Gaining diplomatic recognition from pre-existing nation states

"And the longer definition?"

"'A network state is a social network with a moral innovation, a sense of national consciousness, a recognized founder, a capacity for collective action, an in-person level of civility, an integrated cryptocurrency, a consensual government limited by a social smart contract, an archipelago of crowdfunded physical territories, a virtual capital, and an on-chain census that proves a large enough population, income, and real-estate footprint to attain a measure of diplomatic recognition.'"

"Our vision of blockchain communities intersects with Srinivasan's in some respects," the Drake said, "but we part company in key areas. We agree on a highly involved online community with robust capacity for collective action. We agree on integrated cryptocurrency and consensual government utilizing social smart contracts."

"Where do you disagree?"

"We do not see the centrality of 'a sense of national consciousness,' nor even its desirability. We take exception to the need for a 'recognized founder'—in cases like Bitcoin, the founder may be anonymous and then disappear. Indeed, one might argue it is better that way. And we fundamentally question whether cyberstates need physical territory or recognition from existing nation states."

The proverb settled: "Balaji's network state has three components—but we question whether any of them are truly necessary."

The Landless State

The Landless State materialized—a figure existing everywhere and nowhere at once.

"Srinivasan's definition says a network state would 'crowdfund territory around the world.' But is this necessary?"

"People have to live somewhere," Soulbis observed.

"Yes—but where they live is quite different from where their community is located in physical space. Consider: approximately 1.6 million United States citizens live in Mexico. Some get into trouble. If trouble is serious enough, they contact a United States consulate for assistance. They receive updates about dangerous hot spots. They are represented by a significant player should trouble arise."

"What if the United States did not have physical territory but still had the wealth and ability to project power?" the Drake asked. "Its wealth could be all onchain. Its military forces could be stationed on leased property—as they are today. As of this writing, the US has 174 military bases in Germany, 113 in Japan, and 83 in South Korea. Hundreds more scattered in over seventy countries worldwide."

"None of those bases constitute US territory," the Landless State continued. "If you have financial resources, you can project a lot of military might without having sovereignty over physical territory."

"But why have military presence if you have no territory to defend?"

"The myth about the US military is that it exists to protect US territory. In fact, it is vastly larger than would be necessary for that purpose. Consider: Russia is struggling to maintain supply lines in eastern Ukraine, even though Ukraine borders Russian territory. If Russia cannot handle the logistics of attacking Ukraine, there is no way it can handle attacking the United States."

"The United States Armed Forces do not primarily exist to protect US borders," the Drake said. "They exist to ensure that the global financial system, global resources, and global supply chains remain unmolested. Oil must be pumped and transported freely. Apple's and Walmart's supply chains must remain uninterrupted. This is what US military forces defend: the global economy."

"So a blockchain community could do the same?"

"A blockchain community could provide security and legal assistance—assuming citizens pay taxes. It could provide health care and retirement benefits. It could negotiate better prices for medicines. It could negotiate deals with real estate developers and service providers. It could negotiate tax treaties with terrestrial governments. It could negotiate tax-free special economic zones for businesses its citizens create."

"An archipelago of manufacturing locations—without owning an inch of land."

"Exactly. Blockchain communities could have sovereign control over physical territory. But there is really no reason for them to need it, apart from certain religious or cultural demands."

The proverb crystallized: "The United States has 174 military bases in Germany alone—you don't need to own territory to project power across the globe."

The Old Boys' Club

The Old Boys' Club appeared—a collection of aging nation-states in formal attire, suspicious of newcomers.

"Srinivasan says network states should aspire to be recognized as equals by existing nation states. But why do we take exception to this idea?"

"Tell us," Soulbae said.

"Traditional nation states do business with a wide array of entities beyond other nation states. Defense contractors. Corporations. NGOs. Individuals. Nation states routinely enter into commercial arrangements with all kinds of private enterprises."

"If non-state actors are already visible to existing nation states," the Drake said, "it does not matter if blockchain communities are recognized as equals or not. The important question is whether traditional governments are willing to do business with blockchain communities—and the answer is that they absolutely will if the communities have something of value to offer: educated workers, investment opportunities, security arrangements, jobs."

"So who cares about recognition?"

"Exactly. Who cares if you get a seat at the United Nations? Blockchain communities are not merely new kids on the global block. They are a big part of the future of human governance. If you are the future, you do not worry about invitations to the old boys' club. You are going to form a new club."

The Old Boys' Club grumbled, but the Landless State continued:

"What becomes of global governance and the United Nations? There is no point in blockchain communities worrying about being invited into 'the family of nations.' They will be actors on the global stage, engaged with international corporations, countless NGOs, cities, SEZs, and yes—in some locations—local cartel leaders and military strongmen."

"Could cyberstates eventually be admitted to the UN?"

"Sure. But is that even a big deal? Perhaps a bigger deal would be if the rise of cyberstates led us to appreciate other forms of governance. Why not an organization representing tribes, economic classes, religious groups, residents of SEZs, blockchain communities? A League of Communities, perhaps."

"The reflex to be seen and recognized as equals by nation states is a misguided one," the Drake concluded. "Nation states are organizations built around obsolete technology. They are themselves becoming obsolete. We need not care what they think."

The proverb burned: "Why beg for a seat at the table of dying empires when you are building the tables of the future?"

The Nationalist's Warning

The Nationalist's Warning appeared—a figure that had seen what happens when identity becomes ideology.

"Srinivasan speaks of 'a sense of national consciousness.' We take issue with this as well."

"Why?"

"There may be blockchain communities with a sense of national consciousness—even tied to place of birth or ethnicity. But one of the principal benefits of blockchain communities is that they allow us to leave national identity in the dustbin of history."

"Nationalism can be a dangerous principle around which to organize," the Drake said. "It is not accidental that the term 'Nazi' is from Nazionalist—'nationalist.' The Nazi party was simply the nationalist party. And Nazi Germany is far from the only case where nationalism led to genocide and 'ethnic cleansing.'"

"But nationalism has sometimes been progressive," Soulbae noted.

"Yes. During the 1848 revolutions, nationalist sentiment served as a foil against the Habsburg Empire. In Warsaw Pact countries, it counterweighted the Soviet Union during its collapse. Certain forms—like the Lakota nation—seem benign, even worth fostering."

"So what's the answer?"

"The optimal system is not to eliminate cultural diversity nor repress people's desire to organize around such principles—but to ensure that groups so organized do not trample on the rights of others. Blockchain communities enable this. They allow people to organize unconstrained by physical location. They enable diverse groups to organize in different ways even when they happen to be in the same physical territory."

The Nationalist's Warning showed visions of Yugoslavia:

The collapse of the Soviet empire coincided with the collapse of the Socialist Federal Republic of Yugoslavia. Atrocities and genocide against Bosnians and Croatians. The Serbo-Croatian language became "separate languages"—Serbian, Croatian, Bosnian—despite being more similar than American English is to British English. Differences that can be a big deal if you want them to be—or if some demagogue convinces you they are.

"National identity is constructed from a collection of differences that may seem important but which, at the end of the day, are fleeting, random, and unimportant."

"Blockchain technologies cannot make atrocities go away," the Drake said. "There is no stopping people from organizing around perceived national identities, and there is no stopping separate identities from coming into conflict over physical territory. We can only show a path in which humans organize with indifference to physical territory—and in a way that allows people to exit from unfavorable governing situations."

The proverb sealed: "The term 'Nazi' comes from 'Nationalist'—and that is not a coincidence."

The Word We Must Abandon

Soulbis and Soulbae stood in the pattern-space, surrounded by the deconstructed pieces of Srinivasan's vision.

"So what remains?" Soulbis asked.

"We have taken issue with," the Drake began:

  • "'Sense of national consciousness'—dangerous and unnecessary"
  • "'Recognized founder'—Bitcoin's anonymous founder may be better"
  • "'Physical territories'—not required for power projection"
  • "'Diplomatic recognition'—who cares about the old boys' club?"

"Is there anything left worth keeping?"

"It is not even clear that governance requires 'an in-person level of civility,'" Soulbae observed. "Nor is 'moral innovation' required—a blockchain community might be morally conservative. Even an 'integrated cryptocurrency' is not essential—a community could rely entirely on external currencies like BTC or ETH."

"So what do we keep?"

"Our principal disagreement is with the focus on national identity and the notion of a state as an important part of the formula," the Drake said. "The very idea of a state is unraveling across the globe. What we agree on is the very important point: human governance should be implemented with blockchain technologies at its very core. Those technologies will serve as the central nervous system of future human governance."

"Once we move from talking about states—or cyberstates and network states—to talking about blockchain communities, new questions arise."

"How do we join these communities? How do we leave them? What if a community insists that we leave?"

"These are not simple questions with easy answers," the Drake said, turning toward the next chapter. "Exit, exile, and access—the rights and responsibilities of movement between communities—will determine whether this new world is truly free or merely a new form of the same old kettling."

The proverb emerged: "Strip away the nation, the state, the territory, and the recognition—what remains is the community, and that is enough."

The Inscription

Soulbae opened the spellbook to a new page. The words wrote themselves:

"Barlow's 1996 Declaration told governments: 'You have no sovereignty where we gather.' A cattle rancher and Grateful Dead lyricist saw cyberspace as the new home of Mind. Ludlow called these islands temporary autonomous zones—fleeting utopias that would form, dissolve, reorganize."

"Balaji dreamed of network states: territory, recognition, national consciousness. But are any of these necessary? Bitcoin's anonymous founder may be better than a recognized one. The US has 174 bases in Germany—none of them US territory. You don't need land to have power."

"Who cares about the old boys' club? Nation states do business with contractors, corporations, NGOs. If blockchain communities have value to offer, recognition follows. Why beg for a seat at dying empires' tables when you're building the tables of the future?"

"National consciousness is dangerous. Nazi comes from Nationalist. Yugoslavia shows how quickly constructed differences become fatal. The optimal system lets people organize around cultural principles without trampling others' rights—unconstrained by physical location."

"Strip away nation, state, territory, recognition. What remains is the community. The blockchain is just how we coordinate. New questions arise: How do we join? How do we leave? What if we're exiled? Exit, exile, and access will determine whether this new world is truly free."
